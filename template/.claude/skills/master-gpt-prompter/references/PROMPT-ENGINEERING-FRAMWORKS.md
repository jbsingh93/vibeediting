PROMPT ENGINEERING FRAMWORKS
This is a guide to the various ways to structure a prompt based on what the task demand and
ask for. So
please read this guide carefully and then decide which one to use, based on the frameworks
that would
sovle the user’s need/task/goals.
The FOCUS Framework
Overview: The FOCUS Framework is designed to make AI interactions goal-oriented and
productive. It helps craft prompts that are clear, concise, and relevant.
Components:
1. Function: Define the purpose of the prompt.
2. Objective: Specify the goal of the response.
3. Context: Provide relevant background information.
4. Utility: Explain how the response will be used.
5. Specifications: Detail any requirements for the response.
Example: Generate a report on emerging trends in renewable energy. Focus on
technologies with potential benefits. Include an executive summary, detailed analysis,
examples, and future implications in a PDF format of 10-15 pages.
Strengths:
• Goal-oriented.
• Provides clear expectations.
Weaknesses:
• Requires detailed input.
• Risk of over-specification.
Use Cases: Ideal for content creation, research, strategic planning, and educational
assignments.

The RACE Framework
Overview: The RACE Framework (Reach, Act, Convert, Engage) is designed to achieve
impactful results with AI.
Components:
1. Reach: Define the audience.
2. Act: Specify the action.
3. Convert: Outline the conversion goal.
4. Engage: Describe how to foster interaction.
Example: Improve our social media strategy by analyzing content with high engagement.
Recommend strategies for increased interaction and sharing.
Strengths:
• Comprehensive engagement.
• Strategic clarity.
Weaknesses:
• Complex planning.
• Potential overemphasis on conversion.
Use Cases: Ideal for enhancing social media strategies and marketing campaigns.
Bloom’s Taxonomy Prompts
Overview: Bloom’s Taxonomy categorizes cognitive learning objectives to foster higher-
order thinking.
Components:
1. Remember: Recall information.
2. Understand: Probe comprehension.
3. Apply: Use information in new situations.
4. Analyze: Dissect information.

5. Evaluate: Make judgments.
6. Create: Generate new ideas.
Example: For the water cycle:
1. Remember: List the stages.
2. Understand: Explain how precipitation occurs.
3. Apply: Describe how it affects climate patterns.
4. Analyze: Impact of human activity.
5. Evaluate: Effectiveness of conservation methods.
6. Create: Design a model for water conservation.
Strengths:
• Promotes cognitive development.
• Structured learning objectives.
Weaknesses:
• May require additional training.
• Potential for misapplication.
Use Cases: Ideal for educational settings, curriculum development, and assessment design.
Pros and Cons Analysis
Overview: Pros and Cons Analysis is a straightforward tool for weighing advantages and
disadvantages.
Components:
1. Identify Options: List potential solutions.
2. List Pros: Outline benefits.
3. List Cons: Outline drawbacks.
4. Evaluate and Compare: Assess each option.
Example: For new software implementation:
1. Pros: Increased efficiency, improved data analysis.

2. Cons: High initial cost, training requirements.
Strengths:
• Simple and accessible.
• Facilitates clarity.
Weaknesses:
• Subjectivity.
• Lacks quantitative analysis.
Use Cases: Ideal for personal decisions and organizational choices.
The 3C’s Model for Prompt Engineering
Overview: The 3C’s Model enhances clarity, coherence, and consistency of AI prompts.
Components:
1. Clarity: Make prompts clear and unambiguous.
2. Coherence: Ensure logical flow.
3. Consistency: Maintain style and tone.
Example: For a renewable energy report:
1. Clarity: Provide a detailed report.
2. Coherence: Ensure the report is structured.
3. Consistency: Follow professional standards.
Strengths:
• Enhances comprehension.
• Improves user experience.
Weaknesses:
• May restrict creativity.
• Requires upfront planning.
Use Cases: Ideal for content generation, data analysis reports, and customer service bots.

The 4S Method for Prompt Engineering
Overview: The 4S Method focuses on simplicity, specificity, sensitivity, and structure.
Components:
1. Simplicity: Clear and concise prompts.
2. Specificity: Define specific information needed.
3. Sensitivity: Consider context and implications.
4. Structure: Organize logically.
Example: For social media impact on youth:
1. Simplicity: Analyze influence on mental health.
2. Specificity: Focus on anxiety and self-esteem.
3. Sensitivity: Be sensitive to the subject.
4. Structure: Structure logically.
Strengths:
• Enhanced clarity.
• Targeted outputs.
Weaknesses:
• May require iteration.
• Limited creativity.
Use Cases: Ideal for educational content, customer service bots, and analytical reports.
The APE Framework for Prompt Engineering
Overview: The APE Framework focuses on action, purpose, and expectation.
Components:
1. Action: Specify the task.
2. Purpose: Explain the rationale.
3. Expectation: Describe the desired outcome.
Example: For a marketing strategy:

1. Action: Develop a strategy.
2. Purpose: Maximize market penetration.
3. Expectation: Provide a detailed plan.
Strengths:
• Clear guidance.
• Contextual understanding.
Weaknesses:
• Complex design.
• May limit creativity.
Use Cases: Ideal for content creation, strategic planning, and educational tools.
CAR/PAR/STAR Frameworks
Overview: CAR, PAR, and STAR frameworks articulate challenges, actions, and results.
Components:
1. Challenge/Problem/Situation: Describe the context.
2. Action: Detail actions taken.
3. Result: Highlight outcomes.
Example: For improving team productivity:
1. Challenge: Remote work productivity.
2. Action: Implement virtual team-building.
3. Result: 25% increase in productivity.
Strengths:
• Structured approach.
• Emphasizes action and outcome.
Weaknesses:
• Can be restrictive.
• Requires detailed preparation.

Use Cases: Ideal for interviews, reviews, and leadership training.
The CARE Framework for Prompt Engineering
Overview: The CARE Framework emphasizes context, action, result, and example.
Components:
1. Context: Provide the background.
2. Action: Specify the task.
3. Result: Articulate the desired outcome.
4. Example: Offer an illustrative case.
Example: For improving online customer engagement:
1. Context: Declining interaction.
2. Action: Analyze behavior data.
3. Result: Increase interactions by 20%.
4. Example: Use a case study.
Strengths:
• Enhanced clarity and focus.
• Outcome-oriented.
Weaknesses:
• Increased preparation time.
• Potential over-specification.
Use Cases: Ideal for comprehensive analysis, strategic planning, and educational content.
The "Explain Like I'm 5" (ELI5) Framework
Overview: ELI5 simplifies complex ideas into digestible explanations.
Components:
1. Emphasize Simplicity: Use everyday language.
2. Avoid Jargon: Eliminate technical terms.
3. Use Relatable Examples: Make concepts tangible.

4. Be Concise: Keep explanations brief.
Example: To explain the internet:
1. Emphasize Simplicity: It’s like a magical library.
2. Avoid Jargon: Avoid technical terms.
3. Use Relatable Examples: Talk about cartoons.
4. Be Concise: Keep it brief.
Strengths:
• Universal accessibility.
• Enhances understanding.
Weaknesses:
• Risk of oversimplification.
• May not suit all topics.
Use Cases: Ideal for education, content creation, and customer service.
The Challenge-Solution-Benefit Framework
Overview: This framework articulates problems, solutions, and benefits.
Components:
1. Identify the Challenge: Define the problem.
2. Implement the Solution: Describe actions taken.
3. Highlight the Benefit: Outline positive outcomes.
Example: For customer satisfaction:
1. Challenge: Slow response times.
2. Solution: Implement AI customer service.
3. Benefit: 40% increase in satisfaction.
Strengths:
• Clear structure.
• Effective communication.

Weaknesses:
• May oversimplify.
• Assumes direct causality.
Use Cases: Ideal for case studies, project reports, and marketing.
The COAST Framework for Prompt Engineering
Overview: The COAST Framework covers context, objective, actions, scenario, and task.
Components:
1. Context: Establish the background.
2. Objective: Define the goal.
3. Actions: Specify the actions.
4. Scenario: Describe a situation.
5. Task: Outline the output.
Example: For a digital marketing strategy:
1. Context: New eco-friendly product.
2. Objective: Maximize online visibility.
3. Actions: Analyze market trends.
4. Scenario: Product launch in Q1 2024.
5. Task: Provide a detailed plan.
Strengths:
• Comprehensive structure.
• Enhanced clarity.
Weaknesses:
• Complexity in design.
• May require iteration.
Use Cases: Ideal for content creation, strategic planning, and customer service.

The Elicitation Framework
Overview: The Elicitation Framework refines the art of extracting information from AI.
Components:
1. Clarify Intent: Define the information needed.
2. Structure Questions: Frame open-ended prompts.
3. Contextualize: Provide relevant context.
4. Specify Desired Outcome: Describe the response format.
5. Follow-Up Strategically: Plan for further questions.
Example: To understand the impact of emerging technologies:
1. Clarify Intent: Focus on remote work.
2. Structure Questions: Open-ended analysis.
3. Contextualize: Include industry examples.
4. Specify Desired Outcome: Detailed analysis.
5. Follow-Up Strategically: Address potential impacts.
Strengths:
• Precision in responses.
• Deep insights.
Weaknesses:
• Requires detailed planning.
• Learning curve.
Use Cases: Ideal for market research, academic study, and content creation.
The ERA Framework
Overview: The ERA Framework focuses on expectation, role, and action.
Components:
1. Expectation: Define the desired outcome.
2. Role: Specify the AI's function.

3. Action: Describe the steps to be taken.
Example: For a social media strategy:
1. Expectation: Increase engagement by 30%.
2. Role: Act as a digital marketing consultant.
3. Action: Identify key content themes.
Strengths:
• Clear direction.
• Enhanced focus.
Weaknesses:
• May require iteration.
• Limitations on creativity.
Use Cases: Ideal for content creation, strategic planning, and complex problem-solving.
The Five Ws and One H Framework
Overview: This framework uses who, what, when, where, why, and how to guide AI
responses.
Components:
1. Who: Identify the key players.
2. What: Define the task or subject.
3. When: Specify timing or temporal aspects.
4. Where: Provide geographical context.
5. Why: Articulate the purpose.
6. How: Describe the process.
Example: To explore the impact of remote work:
1. Who: Urban planners and remote workers.
2. What: Impact on urban development.
3. When: Last decade and future projections.

4. Where: Major cities globally.
5. Why: Inform urban planning.
6. How: Analyze development patterns.
Strengths:
• Comprehensive coverage.
• Contextual richness.
Weaknesses:
• Time-consuming.
• Risk of over-specification.
Use Cases: Ideal for research, journalistic inquiry, and business analysis.
The Focused Conversation Method (ORID)
Overview: ORID organizes conversation around objective, reflective, interpretive, and
decisional stages.
Components:
1. Objective: Elicit factual information.
2. Reflective: Encourage reflection on subjective aspects.
3. Interpretive: Guide analysis and interpretation.
4. Decisional: Prompt conclusions or actions.
Example: For digital transformation in small businesses:
1. Objective: Identify key components.
2. Reflective: Reflect on owner sentiments.
3. Interpretive: Discuss future implications.
4. Decisional: Recommend strategies.
Strengths:
• Structured exploration.
• Enhances relevance.

Weaknesses:
• Complex prompt design.
• May limit spontaneity.
Use Cases: Ideal for analysis, decision-making, and educational content.
The GOPA Framework
Overview: GOPA stands for goals, options, pros/cons, and actions.
Components:
1. Goals: Define objectives.
2. Options: List potential actions.
3. Pros/Cons: Evaluate advantages and disadvantages.
4. Actions: Develop a plan based on evaluations.
Example: For expanding market presence:
1. Goals: Increase market share by 20%.
2. Options: New products, digital marketing, new markets.
3. Pros/Cons: Evaluate each option.
4. Actions: Conduct research, allocate budget.
Strengths:
• Structured decision-making.
• Comprehensive analysis.
Weaknesses:
• Time-intensive.
• Risk of overanalysis.
Use Cases: Ideal for strategic planning and business development.
The Hamburger Model for AI Prompt Engineering
Overview: The Hamburger Model structures content with a clear introduction, body, and
conclusion.

Components:
1. Introduction: Set the stage.
2. Body: Elaborate on the topic.
3. Conclusion: Summarize key points.
Example: For discussing renewable energy benefits:
1. Introduction: Address the need for renewable sources.
2. Body: Detail benefits of solar and wind power.
3. Conclusion: Summarize global energy solutions.
Strengths:
• Clear structure.
• Comprehensive exploration.
Weaknesses:
• Potential for formulaic responses.
• Limited flexibility.
Use Cases: Ideal for educational content, structured articles, and business reports.
The PAUSE Framework for Prompt Engineering
Overview: PAUSE encourages reflection and strategic thought in AI interactions.
Components:
1. Pause: Consider the question fully.
2. Assess: Evaluate the situation.
3. Understand: Deepen understanding of the context.
4. Strategize: Contemplate various approaches.
5. Execute: Guide towards concrete actions.
Example: For developing a marketing strategy:
1. Pause: Reflect on target market and features.
2. Assess: Evaluate competitive landscape.

3. Understand: Grasp the product's unique proposition.
4. Strategize: Plan the best outreach methods.
5. Execute: Propose a detailed marketing plan.
Strengths:
• Promotes deeper analysis.
• Enhances decision-making.
Weaknesses:
• May increase response time.
• Requires careful prompt design.
Use Cases: Ideal for strategic business planning and in-depth analysis.
The RISE Framework
Overview: RISE enhances prompt clarity and direction with role, input, steps, and
expectation.
Components:
1. Role: Define the AI's role.
2. Input: Specify necessary information.
3. Steps: Outline a series of actions.
4. Expectation: Describe the desired outcome.
Example: For analyzing renewable energy adoption:
1. Role: Evaluate energy trends.
2. Input: Use global reports and policy changes.
3. Steps: Compare energy sources and project future trends.
4. Expectation: Deliver a comprehensive report.
Strengths:
• Structured approach.
• Comprehensive exploration.

Weaknesses:
• Complexity in prompt design.
• Potential for over-specification.
Use Cases: Ideal for research analysis and strategic planning.
The SMART Framework
Overview: SMART focuses on specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound
elements.
Components:
1. Specific: Clarify the prompt.
2. Measurable: Set assessment criteria.
3. Achievable: Ensure feasibility.
4. Relevant: Align with goals.
5. Time-bound: Define a timeframe.
Example: For optimizing a marketing campaign:
1. Specific: Analyze engagement rates.
2. Measurable: Calculate percentages.
3. Achievable: Focus on realistic adjustments.
4. Relevant: Prioritize effective platforms.
5. Time-bound: Apply insights immediately.
Strengths:
• Precision and clarity.
• Efficiency.
Weaknesses:
• Potential for rigidity.
• Preparation time.
Use Cases: Ideal for business strategy and content creation.

Chain of Thought Framework
Overview: This framework encourages a logical sequence in problem-solving with AI.
Components:
1. Introduction: Present the issue.
2. Breakdown: Dissect the problem.
3. Logical Progression: Connect steps logically.
4. Conclusion: Synthesize insights.
Example: For economic impacts of renewable energy:
1. Introduction: Address global impacts.
2. Breakdown: Consider costs and effects on employment.
3. Logical Progression: Link costs to benefits.

4. Conclusion: Summarize economic growth potential.
Strengths:
• Enhances AI's reasoning.
• Promotes depth and clarity.
Weaknesses:
• Requires precise prompting.
• May be time-consuming.
Use Cases: Ideal for analytical challenges and detailed explorations.
Chain of Destiny Framework
Overview: This framework uses iterative refinement to enhance AI-generated content.
Components:
1. Initial Prompt: Start with a baseline.
2. Iteration: Implement refinements.
3. Feedback: Provide targeted improvements.
4. Refinement: Gradually enhance output.
Example: For optimizing a blog on sustainable living:
1. Initial Prompt: Draft a post on sustainable practices.
2. Iteration: Suggest more specific examples.
3. Feedback: Improve explanations and evidence.
4. Refinement: Focus on clarity and engaging advice.
Strengths:
• Continuous improvement.
• Customized feedback.
Weaknesses:
• Time-intensive.
• Depends on quality feedback.

Use Cases: Ideal for projects where refinement enhances quality.
